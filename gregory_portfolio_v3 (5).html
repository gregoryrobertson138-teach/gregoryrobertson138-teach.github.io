Professional Teaching Portfolio

Hello! My name is Gregory Robertson, and I am a secondary English Language Arts educator currently teaching 10th and 12th grade at Non Traditional Program South, a Prince George's County Public Schools.

I am currently pursuing my Master of Arts in Teaching at Morgan State University, where I am focused on my professional development as a secondary English Language Arts educator.

My teaching practice is grounded in culturally relevant pedagogy. I believe students engage most deeply when they see themselves and their communities reflected in the texts we study together. I specialize in pairing canonical literature including Shakespeare with contemporary music, art, and cultural texts to build meaningful connections for all learners.

I am committed to differentiated instruction and ensuring that all students, including those with IEPs and 504 plans, have full access to rigorous, meaningful learning experiences.

I am glad you are here. This portfolio is a reflection of my growth, my students, and the work I show up to do every day. I hope it gives you a real sense of who I am as an educator.

School Demographics & Classroom Description

I teach at Non Traditional Program South, an alternative placement school within Prince George's County Public Schools. PGCPS is one of the largest school districts in Maryland. NTP South serves students who have been placed in an alternative setting, many of whom carry significant external stressors into the building each day. The student population is predominantly African American, and the school faces serious challenges around attendance and behavior that reflect broader systemic inequities rather than individual student failure.

My Classroom

I teach 10th and 12th grade English Language Arts at NTP South. My students come to me with a wide range of academic experiences, many of them having faced interruptions to their schooling. My classes include students with IEPs for Specific Learning Disabilities including dyslexia, students with 504 plans for ADHD, and English Language Learners. The non traditional setting means that many of my students are also managing hybrid schedules, working jobs, or dealing with circumstances outside of school that make consistent attendance and focus difficult. Every instructional decision I make is shaped by that reality. I design my lessons to meet students where they are, build on their strengths, and connect content to their lives so that school feels worth showing up to.

Teaching Philosophy

Reasons to Teach & Definition of Good Teaching

Growing up, my lifelong dream was not to be in the field of education. My true thoughts and beliefs about education were that it was boring, and it did not fit my aesthetic or lifestyle choice. When I was in grade school, teachers would always ask me what I wanted to be, and I always said I wanted to be financially stable. My father made it very evident in my life that he wanted nothing but success for me. It took my senior year of college to understand what that would look like. With that expectation, it pushed me toward the field of education and being more proactive about my career. In elementary school, I had one teacher who made a significant impact on my life, Mr. Thomas, who made it his mission to connect with us socially and emotionally. That connection went far beyond the lens of the classroom or the subject matter. Being a respectable and good teacher means being that person in the building that students can learn from, lean on, and look at. It means showing up when others might not show up for those students, meeting them where they are, and drawing out what they might not yet see in themselves.

Educational Philosophy

My teaching philosophy is rooted in culturally relevant pedagogy, a framework developed by Dr. Gloria Ladson-Billings that centers the cultural identities, lived experiences, and community knowledge that students bring into the classroom. I was introduced to this framework through my coursework at Morgan State University, and it immediately resonated with me based on my own experiences and what I had witnessed in schools. When students see themselves reflected in the material their music, their language, their histories they do not simply tolerate learning; they invest in it. Culturally relevant pedagogy pushes back against passive memorization and surface-level compliance. It asks me to go beyond the standard curriculum to connect with student interests and create an engaging classroom environment. In my practice, I have noticed that when students feel respected for who they are inside and outside the classroom, they engage. That connection runs deeper than any traditional approach to instruction could achieve on its own.

Teaching Methods, Classroom Environment & Technology Integration

One of the greatest advantages I have discovered in my practice is working in a smaller classroom setting. Rather than spending energy managing a room full of behaviors, I can invest that energy into building relationships and those relationships go a long way. My methods center on being culturally relevant to my students: listening to what they talk about, paying attention to what they consume, and regularly anchoring academic content to current events, film, and lived experience. For example, I have paired Shakespeare with Beyoncé and Frank Ocean alongside poetry to help students explore tone and voice through lenses they already understand. Technology integration looks like using discussion platforms and educational games to meet students where they are and sustain engagement in meaningful ways. One key takeaway from this program has been allowing student choice during instruction to build genuine buy-in. I read the room during instruction, ask targeted questions, and maintain a classroom environment where students feel comfortable being wrong, taking risks, and growing. Even the physical layout of my classroom with students facing one another reflects my philosophy, as it encourages authentic discussion and community-centered learning.

Evaluation & Assessment Methods

My approach to evaluation has significantly changed since joining my master's program at Morgan State University. In my first two years of teaching, I focused primarily on traditional assessment methods, but working with my student population at a non-traditional program has pushed me to rethink what meaningful assessment looks like. I have shifted toward performance-based assessments with written analyses, projects, and exit tickets because they ask students to demonstrate understanding rather than simply recall information. In my experience, students have responded best to project-based learning as a vehicle for showing what they have genuinely internalized. Formative assessment is constant in my classroom; I am continuously collecting informal data through targeted questioning and ongoing checks for understanding. Summative assessments are designed to give students choice so they can take ownership of how they demonstrate mastery, which aligns with culturally relevant pedagogy and honors the diversity of my learners. For students with disabilities, I provide scaffolding and modified task structures without ever lowering academic expectations, because every student deserves access to rigor.

Teaching Goals & Plans for Professional Growth

My primary goal is to strengthen students' ability to understand literature, expand comprehension levels, and develop the analytical vocabulary needed to communicate ideas clearly whether through writing or classroom discussion. I want students to leave my classroom not only able to read a text closely but also willing to question it, connect it to their own world, and form independent positions. Beyond academic skills, I want students to leave with a stronger sense of their own voice and the confidence to use it. The impression I hope to leave is one where students understand that their opinions matter and that speaking with confidence is both a right and a skill. My plans for professional growth include refining my differentiated instruction strategies, strengthening my ability to push students toward expanded thinking, and continuing to develop my data literacy so I can respond more precisely to where individual students are. I also plan to expand and broaden the range of culturally relevant texts in my curriculum so that the classroom continues to feel like a space built for and belonging to the students inside it.

Standard 3

Learning Environments

The teacher works with others to create environments that support individual and collaborative learning, encouraging positive social interaction and self-motivation.

Artifact

Cougar Time as a Restorative SEL Intervention — Action Research Study, NTP South PGCPS

Rationale

My school is not a traditional setting. A lot of my students come in already stressed out, and that makes learning hard. Cougar Time was a program we put in place to give students a break at the end of the day to do something they actually wanted to do. After it launched, behavioral incidents dropped 42%. But what stuck with me more was what students said about it. They said it made them feel like school actually cared about them. That is the kind of environment I want to be part of building.

Standard 4

Content Knowledge

The teacher understands the central concepts and tools of inquiry of the discipline and creates learning experiences that make the discipline accessible and meaningful.

Artifact

Antony and Cleopatra Daily Lesson Plan Cleopatra's "Immortal Longings" Speech (Act 5, Sc. 2)

Rationale

This lesson demonstrates my deep knowledge of Shakespeare's language, imagery, and dramatic structure. I designed think-alouds focused on Cleopatra's diction, metaphor, and figurative language guiding students through phrases like "I am fire and air" and "stroke of death is as a lover's pinch" to uncover meaning, tone, and character motivation. The lesson is aligned to CCSS RL.11-12.1, RL.11-12.2, and RL.11-12.4, and moves students from surface reading to analytical interpretation of one of Shakespeare's most complex speeches.

Standard 5

Application of Content

Artifact

Module 2, Lesson 3: Cleopatra in Music — Frank Ocean "Pyramids," The Lumineers "The Ballad of Cleopatra," Tal Bachman "She's So High"

Music is something my students already speak. So after we read Cleopatra's speech I played them songs by Frank Ocean, The Lumineers, and Tal Bachman that were all inspired by her character. Standard 7

Planning for Instruction

The teacher plans instruction that supports every student in meeting rigorous learning goals by drawing upon knowledge of content areas, curriculum, and pedagogy.

Artifact

edTPA Lesson Plan Sequence Antony and Cleopatra Unit (Lessons 1 3)

Rationale

When I planned this unit I wanted each lesson to build on the one before it. We started with something familiar by connecting Shakespeare to Beyonce so students would not shut down right away. Then we moved into reading and discussing the actual speeches. By the third lesson students were doing the analysis more on their own. That progression was intentional. I did not just throw lessons together. I thought about where students were starting and where I needed them to get to.

Standard 8

Instructional Strategies

The teacher understands and uses a variety of instructional strategies to encourage learners to develop deep understanding and build skills to apply knowledge.

Artifact

Antony and Cleopatra Lesson Plan Instructional Strategies: Think-Aloud, Graphic Organizer, Small Group Discussion, Written Response

Rationale

This lesson demonstrates my use of multiple, intentionally sequenced instructional strategies. I open with a teacher think-aloud to model literary analysis, then move students into small-group discussion using a Big Ideas graphic organizer to build collaborative understanding. A whole-class debrief follows with targeted guiding questions before students complete an independent written response. This gradual release structure I do, we do, you do ensures students are never working beyond their supported capacity while still being pushed toward independent analytical thinking.
